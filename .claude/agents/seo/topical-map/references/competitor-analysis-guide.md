# Competitor Analysis Guide

This document provides a framework for analyzing lookalike competitors to inform the topical map.

## Purpose of Competitor Analysis

Competitor analysis in topical mapping serves to:

1. **Validate pillar choices** — Confirm competitors target similar topics
2. **Discover cluster opportunities** — Find topics competitors rank for that should be covered
3. **Identify content gaps** — Find topics competitors miss or cover poorly
4. **Learn content formats** — Understand what content types work in the space

## Competitor Selection Criteria

The user provides 2-5 lookalike competitors. Validate that competitors are appropriate:

### Good Competitors
- Similar size/stage or slightly larger
- Target similar audience
- Offer comparable products/services
- Have established content/SEO presence

### Less Useful Competitors
- Massive enterprises (if the user is a startup)
- Different target market
- No content marketing presence
- Pure paid acquisition players

If the provided competitors seem misaligned, suggest alternatives or note limitations.

## Analysis Process

### Step 1: Search Competitor Brand + Offering

For each competitor, run web searches:

- `[Competitor name]` — understand their positioning
- `[Competitor name] [main offering]` — find their core product pages
- `[Competitor name] blog` or `[Competitor name] resources` — find their content

**Look for:**
- What topics appear in their top-ranking content
- How they structure their content (blog categories, resource hubs)
- What content types they produce (guides, comparisons, tools)

### Step 2: Search Keywords to Find Competitor Rankings

For each pillar keyword identified, search:

- `[Pillar keyword]` — see which competitors rank
- `[Pillar keyword] [competitor name]` — find specific competitor content

When a competitor appears in results:
1. Note that they rank for this topic
2. Use web_fetch to visit their ranking page
3. Analyze the page content structure and depth

**Document:**
- Does competitor have content for this pillar? Yes/No
- What specific pages rank?
- What angle do they take?
- What subtopics do they cover?

### Step 3: Deep-Dive Competitor Content

For high-priority keywords where competitors rank, use web_fetch to analyze their pages:

- What topics and subtopics do they cover?
- How is the content structured (headings, sections)?
- What questions do they answer?
- What's missing or could be done better?

**Note:**
- Which competitors appear in results
- What content type ranks (blog, landing page, tool)
- Quality/depth of top-ranking content

### Step 4: Identify Gaps

Gaps are opportunities where:

1. **No competitor covers the topic** — Uncontested opportunity
2. **Competitor coverage is thin** — Opportunity to create better content
3. **Competitor coverage is outdated** — Opportunity for fresh content
4. **Different format could win** — If all competitors have text, maybe video or tool would rank

## Competitive Positioning Insights

After analyzing all competitors, synthesize insights:

### Content Opportunities

| Opportunity | Why It Matters | Recommended Approach |
|-------------|----------------|---------------------|
| [Gap topic] | No competitor coverage | Create comprehensive guide |
| [Weak coverage topic] | Competitor content is thin | Create deeper resource |
| [Format opportunity] | All competitors use [X] | Try [Y] format instead |

### Competitive Advantages to Leverage

Based on competitor analysis, identify angles the user's business can own:

- **Expertise angle** — If competitors lack depth, go deeper
- **Freshness angle** — If competitor content is dated, publish current
- **Format angle** — If competitors use long text, try visual or interactive
- **Audience angle** — If competitors are generic, niche down
- **Geographic angle** — If competitors are national, go local (or vice versa)

## Warning Signs

Note if competitor analysis reveals challenges:

| Warning | What It Means | Recommendation |
|---------|---------------|----------------|
| All top competitors are enterprise giants | Very competitive space | Focus on long-tail, niche pillars first |
| No competitors have content presence | May indicate low search demand | Validate demand with search volume proxies |
| Competitors use primarily paid acquisition | SEO may not be primary channel | Consider if organic is right strategy |

## Output for Topical Map

The competitor analysis should inform:

1. **Pillar validation** — Confirm or adjust pillar choices based on competitor focus
2. **Cluster additions** — Add clusters discovered from competitor content
3. **Priority adjustments** — Increase priority for gap opportunities
4. **Content type recommendations** — Specify formats based on what works
5. **Competitive insights section** — Include key findings in final deliverable
